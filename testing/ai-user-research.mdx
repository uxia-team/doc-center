AI User Research lets you gather deep qualitative feedback from synthetic audiences by asking them direct questions — about your product, your messaging, your concepts, or anything else you need to understand. Instead of navigating an interface, testers respond to your questions in their own words, giving you the _why_ behind user behavior at a scale and speed that traditional research can't match.

## How this differs from an AI User Test

<Note>
  AI User Research and AI User Tests are complementary, not interchangeable. An **AI User Test** observes how testers _navigate and interact_ with an interface — it's behavioral. **AI User Research** asks testers _what they think, feel, and prefer_ — it's attitudinal. Use research to validate ideas and understand opinions; use testing to identify UX friction and usability issues.
</Note>

## Question types supported

Build your research study using four flexible question formats:

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Open-Ended" icon="comment-dots">
    Ask testers to share thoughts, describe experiences, or explain their reasoning in their own words. Best for uncovering unexpected insights and understanding motivations.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Rating Scales" icon="star-half-stroke">
    Collect structured sentiment and satisfaction scores. Use Likert scales or numeric ratings to quantify how testers feel about a concept, message, or experience.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Multiple Choice" icon="list-check">
    Present defined options and see which resonates most. Useful for comparing concepts, testing preferences, or segmenting responses by choice.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Yes / No" icon="circle-check">
    Get a clear binary signal on specific claims, features, or value propositions. Fast to run and easy to analyze across large synthetic audiences.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## How to set up an AI User Research study

<Steps>
  <Step title="Create a new research study">
    From your Uxia dashboard, select **AI User Research** and give your study a name. Describe the topic or product area you want to explore.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Define your synthetic audience">
    Configure who you want to hear from. Set demographics (age, location, occupation), psychographic attributes (attitudes, motivations, domain familiarity), or upload a persona file or customer data via Audience Enrichment to build an audience that reflects your real users.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Write your questions">
    Add your questions using the supported formats — open-ended, rating scales, multiple choice, or yes/no. Mix and match types to get both quantitative signal and qualitative depth in a single study.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Launch the study">
    Send your study to your synthetic audience. Responses are generated immediately — no recruiting, scheduling, or incentive management required.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Review responses and dig deeper">
    Read through responses in your dashboard. Use the chat feature to follow up with individual testers and probe for more detail. Download a shareable insights report to distribute findings to your team.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Use cases

AI User Research is built for the moments when you need to understand what people think — before you build, between iterations, or when behavior data alone isn't enough:

- **Validate product ideas** before committing to a roadmap or sprint
- **Test marketing messages** to find the framing that lands with your target audience
- **Run concept testing** to compare multiple directions and pick the strongest one
- **Compare audience segments** to understand how different groups respond to the same prompt
- **Uncover customer needs** that your current product isn't addressing
- **Understand the _why_** behind patterns you're already seeing in analytics or testing

## Audience Enrichment

Audience Enrichment lets you build synthetic audiences that reflect your real users. Upload your own customer data — survey responses, interview notes, CRM attributes, or behavioral profiles — and Uxia uses it to construct synthetic testers who think and respond like the people you're actually trying to reach. The result is qualitative feedback that's grounded in your specific audience, not a generic population.

## Chat with your testers

After testers respond to your questions, you can open a conversation with any individual tester to dig deeper. Ask follow-up questions, probe for more detail, push back on a response, or explore an objection further. This gives you the flexibility of a one-on-one interview at the scale of a quantitative study — you can talk to as many testers as you need, whenever it's useful.

## Frequently asked questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="How is a synthetic audience different from a real survey panel?">
    A synthetic audience is made up of AI-generated testers rather than recruited human participants. They're available immediately, require no incentives, and can be configured to match any demographic or psychographic profile. When you use Audience Enrichment to ground them in your real customer data, their responses closely mirror what your actual users would say.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Can I use AI User Research to test concepts before I build anything?">
    Yes — concept testing is one of the most common uses. You can describe a product idea, share a value proposition, or present a rough concept in plain language, then ask synthetic testers how they'd respond. This gives you directional signal before you invest in design or development.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="What is Audience Enrichment and how do I use it?">
    Audience Enrichment is a feature that lets you upload your own user data to inform how synthetic testers are constructed. You can provide survey responses, interview transcripts, CRM attributes, or any other data that describes your real customers. Uxia uses that input to shape synthetic testers who behave and respond more like your specific audience rather than a generic one.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Can I combine AI User Research with an AI User Test?">
    Yes. Research and testing answer different questions, and running both gives you a fuller picture. Use research to understand attitudes and preferences, then use an AI User Test to see how those attitudes play out when testers actually interact with your product.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
