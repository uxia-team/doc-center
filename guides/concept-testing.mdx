The most expensive mistake in product development is building the wrong thing with confidence. Concept testing lets you pressure-test ideas — product directions, feature names, value propositions, landing page copy — with the people who matter most, before you commit engineering time. With Uxia's AI User Research, you get structured qualitative and quantitative feedback from synthetic audiences matched to your target market in minutes, not weeks.

## What You Can Concept Test

Uxia's AI User Research is designed for discovery and validation work that happens before or between builds:

- **Product ideas** — is this problem worth solving? Does this solution resonate?
- **Feature names and labels** — does this terminology mean what you think it means to your users?
- **Value propositions** — which benefit statement lands hardest with your audience?
- **Marketing messages and taglines** — which framing drives interest or trust?
- **Landing page copy** — does the headline communicate your offer clearly?
- **Pricing framing** — how do users perceive your pricing structure or tier names?
- **Onboarding flows** — do new users understand what they're being asked to do and why?

## AI User Research vs. AI User Test

<Note>
  Use **AI User Research** when you want to understand opinions, motivations, and preferences — before or without an interface to interact with. Use **AI User Test** when you want to observe how users interact with an interface. Research uncovers *why* and *whether*; testing reveals *where* and *how*.
</Note>

## Step-by-Step Guide

<Steps>
  <Step title="Create a new AI User Research test">
    From your Uxia dashboard, click **New Test** and select **AI User Research**. Give your test a name that reflects the concept or question you're investigating — for example, "Value prop validation — SMB segment" or "Feature name test — v3 onboarding."
  </Step>

  <Step title="Define your target audience">
    Set the demographic and behavioral profile for the people whose opinion matters most for this concept. If you're testing a B2B SaaS idea, specify job roles, company sizes, and tech familiarity. If you're validating a consumer product, set age, interests, and purchase behaviors. The tighter the audience definition, the more signal you get from each response.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Write your questions">
    Start with open-ended questions to surface unprompted reactions and mental models. Follow with rating scales or multiple-choice questions to get quantitative validation. See the question-writing tips and cheat sheet below for guidance.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Select testers">
    Review available synthetic tester profiles and select those that best match your defined audience. For concept testing, 5–10 testers typically provides enough qualitative depth to identify patterns without noise.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Launch and read results">
    Click **Launch**. Results arrive in minutes. Read the **Insights Report** for synthesized themes and standout quotes, then review individual tester responses to understand the full range of reactions.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Ask follow-up questions using the chat feature">
    Once results are in, use Uxia's chat feature to ask follow-up questions about your findings. Probe deeper on a surprising response, ask for clarification on a pattern, or explore a new angle that emerged from the initial results — without running a new test.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Writing Good Research Questions

The quality of your questions determines the quality of your insights. Weak questions produce polite, useless answers. Strong questions surface real opinions.

<Tip>
  **Start open-ended.** Ask "How do you currently handle X?" before asking "Would you pay for a tool that handles X?" Open questions reveal mental models and language your users actually use — gold for copywriting and positioning.
</Tip>

<Tip>
  **Use scenario framing.** Put the tester in a realistic context before asking for their opinion. "Imagine you've just landed on this page for the first time. What's your immediate reaction?" produces richer responses than "What do you think of this headline?"
</Tip>

<Tip>
  **Avoid leading questions.** "How easy was it to understand our value proposition?" assumes it was understandable. Try "What, if anything, do you understand this product to do?" instead.
</Tip>

<Tip>
  **Save yes/no and rating questions for the end.** Use them to quantify patterns you've already identified through open-ended responses — not to fish for validation at the start.
</Tip>

## Question Type Cheat Sheet

| Question Type | Best For | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Open-ended** | Motivation, objections, mental models | "Walk me through how you currently handle X." |
| **Rating scale** | Measuring satisfaction or confidence | "How likely are you to trust this page? (1–5)" |
| **Multiple choice** | Comparing options or preferences | "Which headline resonates more with you?" |
| **Yes/No** | Quick validation | "Would you click this CTA?" |
